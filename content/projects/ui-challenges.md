## Overview

These three UI design challenges emerged from frustration with apps I use daily. Rather than accept dated interfaces or confusing workflows, I redesigned them—exploring how small, intentional changes to layout, hierarchy, and visual language can fundamentally improve the experience.

Each challenge started with the same question: *What if this app felt modern, clear, and genuinely helpful?*

The goal wasn't to overhaul entire ecosystems, but to refine and modernize—to show how respecting the existing functionality while upgrading the visual language creates something that feels both familiar and elevated.

---

## Challenge 1: Sagefund — Sustainable investing made tangible

### The context

Sagefund is a sustainable investing platform designed to make it easier to jump into the world of investing while focusing on sustainability and ease of use. The app guides users through their investment journey, showing the real-world impact of their choices.

### The challenge

The original interface presented impact data clinically—charts and numbers without context. Users could log their investment activity, but the experience didn't *feel* like they were making a meaningful choice. There was a disconnect between "I'm putting money somewhere" and "I'm actually moving the needle on sustainability."

The design needed to bridge that gap: make sustainability feel tangible, not abstract. And make investment tracking feel like progress, not accounting.

### The solution

![App 1](/media/projects/ui-challenges/Sagefund.png)

**Screen 1: Impact-first dashboard**

The redesigned home screen puts impact front and center. Instead of burying sustainability metrics in a secondary section, they become the primary narrative. Users see:

- **Welcome message** with personalized context ("Hi Alex, here's your impact so far")
- **Illustrated impact stories** (carbon offset, environmental progress) with real numbers attached—not just data, but meaning
- **Temperature impact** displayed as a tangible metric users can understand and track
- **Action-oriented CTAs** ("See impact," "Update details") that guide next steps

The color palette (teal and green) reinforces the sustainability mission without feeling preachy. Illustrations add personality and make the interface feel supportive rather than institutional.

**Screen 2: Investment management with clarity**

The second screen handles the practical side: changing investments and tracking portfolio progress. The redesign simplifies this:

- **Clear balance display** at the top—users know their financial position at a glance
- **Investment breakdown** showing what's in the portfolio and how it's performing
- **Status indicators** ("On track") provide confidence and reassurance
- **Easy modification** with prominent CTAs for updating investments
- **Contextual information** about performance and impact, always visible

The interface respects the user's dual concerns: financial growth *and* sustainability impact. Neither is hidden; both inform decisions.

### Key design decisions

- Impact data elevated from secondary to primary—sustainability is the story, not a feature
- Illustrations and color language reinforce brand values and emotional connection
- Clear visual hierarchy guides users from "where am I?" to "what's my next action?"
- Financial and impact data coexist without competing for attention
- Personality in design (illustrations, tone) makes investing feel less intimidating

## Challenge 2: WIZ — Smart home control redesigned

### The context

WIZ is a smart home application that lets users control connected devices—lights, thermostats, security systems—from a single interface. It's a practical tool for managing your home, but the interface had grown cluttered over time as features accumulated.

### The challenge

The original design tried to surface everything at once: device controls, scenes, schedules, all competing for space. Users struggling to find a simple action—turning on a light—had to navigate through a visually noisy interface. The app worked, but it didn't feel intuitive or responsive.

The core issue: **context was missing**. Users didn't know which device was which, what state it was in, or what actions made sense in the current moment.

### The solution

![App 1](/media/projects/ui-challenges/wiz.png)

**Restructured control center with intelligent defaults**

The redesign reorganizes the home screen around the user's mental model:

- **Device-centric layout** with visual grouping—lights, thermostats, security systems are clearly distinguished
- **Status-aware controls** showing whether each device is ON/OFF and its current state
- **Contextual icons and colors** that communicate function immediately (yellow for lights, blue for security, green for smart features)
- **Button repositioning** places frequently-used controls (lights, temperature) in the primary zone, with "Add device" and less frequent actions secondary
- **Simplified visual hierarchy** removes clutter while maintaining all functionality


![App 1](/media/projects/ui-challenges/wiz.gif)

**Small changes, big impact**

The improvements seem modest individually, but compound:
- Icons now clearly indicate what device is being controlled
- Toggle states (ON/OFF) are unmistakable
- Color-coding creates instant visual recognition
- Spacing and grouping reduce cognitive load
- The interface feels responsive and fast

### Key design decisions

- Controls reorganized around user mental model (devices and rooms, not arbitrary sections)
- Visual feedback (colors, icons, states) communicates clearly without text overload
- Frequently-used actions elevated; advanced features remain accessible but not intrusive
- Consistent visual language across all device controls
- Responsive design updates feel snappy and reliable

## Challenge 3: ABN AMRO — Modern banking fundamentals

### The context

ABN AMRO is a major banking application used daily by thousands for account management, transfers, and financial overview. The design had aged, and the information architecture scattered key features across screens.

### The challenge

The original dashboard buried the most important information—account balance, recent transactions, spending patterns—beneath layers of navigation. Users came to the app to quickly check their balance or make a transfer, but had to dig through clunky layouts to find what they needed.

The core problem: **information hierarchy was backwards**. What users needed most was hardest to find.

### The solution

![App 1](/media/projects/ui-challenges/abn.png)

**Redesigned dashboard with progressive disclosure**

The new design inverts the information hierarchy:

**Left screen: Full account view**
- **Balance prominently displayed** at the top—users know their financial position immediately
- **Account summary** showing all accounts and balances in one organized view
- **Transaction history** with clear categorization and date ranges
- **Action-centric footer** with quick access to core banking functions

**Right screens: Focused views with intelligent organization**

- **Screen 2 (Accounts view)**: Shows all accounts with balances and a visual breakdown of where money lives
  - Recent transactions (Primark, Tim's coffee) appear in "Earlier this week" and "This month" sections
  - Users can drill down into categories without losing context
  - Visual icons (clothing, food, parking) add at-a-glance understanding of spending

- **Screen 3 (Alternative layout)**: Demonstrates flexibility—same information, different organization
  - Transaction categories grouped by time period
  - Spending patterns visible without opening detailed reports
  - All core actions (transfer, schedule, settings) remain accessible

### Visual and interactive improvements

- **Teal and green palette** feels modern and trustworthy (banking without coldness)
- **Clear typography hierarchy** distinguishes account names from balances from transaction details
- **Icon-based categorization** helps users instantly recognize spending patterns
- **Whitespace** and card-based layout reduce visual fatigue
- **Consistent interaction patterns** across all screens build confidence

### Key design decisions

- Balance and account information elevated to primary real estate
- Transaction history reorganized by time and category for quick scanning
- Visual icons communicate spending patterns without text overload
- Action buttons remain accessible without cluttering the dashboard
- Modern color language and typography feel current while maintaining trust
- Progressive disclosure allows quick answers and deeper exploration

## Common threads across all three challenges

### 1. **Information hierarchy**
Each redesign started by asking: What does the user need *first*? The current designs often buried the answer. The redesigns elevate core information—impact, device status, account balance—to where users look first.

### 2. **Visual clarity through organization**
Whether grouping investment impacts, device controls, or transaction categories, the solution was always the same: organize information according to user mental models, not system logic.

### 3. **Personality without clutter**
Each redesign adds warmth through color, typography, and illustration—but never at the expense of clarity. Modern design language replaces dated interfaces while maintaining professionalism.

### 4. **Context and feedback**
Users need to understand their current state and what happens next. Status indicators (ON/OFF, "On track"), visual feedback (colors, icons), and clear CTAs make the interface feel responsive and trustworthy.

### 5. **Respecting existing workflows**
These weren't complete redesigns or rebellious reimaginings. They were refinements—modernizing while respecting what already worked, improving the journey without starting over.

---

## Design principles applied

Across all three challenges, several consistent principles emerged:

**Hierarchy matters**: Put the most important information first. Users should understand their situation in under three seconds.

**Visual language communicates**: Icons, colors, and typography do heavy lifting. They reduce reliance on text while adding personality.

**Context is king**: Users need to understand where they are, what state things are in, and what action makes sense next. Transparency about current state reduces friction.

**Defaults are powerful**: Smart defaults (suggested investments, device grouping, transaction categorization) guide users without removing choice.

**Consistency builds confidence**: When patterns repeat across screens, users build mental models faster. Inconsistency creates confusion.

## Reflection

These three challenges demonstrated that modernization doesn't require starting from scratch. Real impact comes from:

- Understanding user intent and designing backward from that
- Applying consistent visual language and interaction patterns
- Respecting the core offering while refining the experience
- Adding personality without sacrificing clarity

Each app I used daily had potential—the bones were solid. They just needed a visual and structural refresh to feel modern, supportive, and genuinely helpful.

The real lesson: small, intentional improvements to hierarchy, clarity, and visual language compound into experiences that feel entirely new—even when they're fundamentally the same product.
