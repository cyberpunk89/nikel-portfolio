## Overview

Sirelo connects people with moving companies — a platform where the core action is simple: request a quote, find a mover, get on with your life. But the homepage was getting in the way of that. Cluttered layout, confusing navigation, a value proposition buried under too much content. The redesign set out to fix all of it.

**The goal:** Increase user engagement, improve conversion rates, and clear the path to quote requests.

I was the sole UX/UI designer on this, working alongside a product manager and an SEO specialist. Three people, one focused brief, and a homepage that needed a serious rethink.

---

## The problem

Sirelo's homepage wasn't short on content — moving guides, pricing information, visa tips, healthcare considerations. All genuinely useful. But the sheer volume of it was working against the platform. Users landing on the page were getting pulled in too many directions at once, and the thing Sirelo most needed them to do — request a quote — was getting lost in the noise.

Hotjar analysis made the friction visible. The combination of a secondary navigation menu and a cluttered hero section was creating a chaotic first impression. Users weren't finding the CTA. Some weren't even registering it.

![Previous homepage — hero section and secondary navigation](/media/projects/sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey/sirelo-old-homepage.gif)

Two problems sat at the root of it:

**1. A hero section doing too much.**
The illustration in the hero section dominated the visual real estate without directing attention anywhere useful. The CTA — the most important element on the page — blended into the background rather than standing out from it.

**2. Secondary navigation creating the wrong kind of choice.**
The navigation banks near the top of the page pulled users sideways before they'd even understood what the page was offering. Good content, wrong placement.
![Previous homepage — hero section and secondary navigation](/media/projects/sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey/sirelo-old-homepage.webp)
---

## Business aim and UX goal

The business objective was straightforward: make the CTA more prominent and drive more quote requests. Every design decision needed to serve that.

The UX goal was the complement to that: build a homepage that communicates Sirelo's value proposition clearly and immediately, so users understand what the platform does and feel confident taking the next step.

Both goals pointed in the same direction — less clutter, more clarity.

---

## Design phase

### Mapping the old IA

Before touching any layouts, I mapped out the existing information architecture to get a clear picture of what was there and how it was ordered.

![Old homepage information architecture](/media/projects/sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey/sirelo-old-ia.webp)

The IA made the problem tangible. The page was revealing Sirelo's services gradually — a slow unveiling that asked users to do too much interpretive work before they got to the point. By the time the value proposition became clear, many users had already moved on.

Three specific issues emerged from the analysis:

**Cluttered hero section** — The illustration consumed space without guiding the eye. The CTA was competing with too many elements and losing.

**Secondary navigation confusion** — Navigation banks near the top diverted users from the core task before they'd oriented themselves on the page.

**Vague value proposition** — The page's structure delayed clarity. Users leaving the page without requesting a quote was a symptom of not immediately understanding why they should.

---

### Building the new IA

The redesigned IA was built around one question: what does a user need to understand, and in what order, to feel confident requesting a quote?

![New Sirelo homepage information architecture](/media/projects/sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey/sirelo-new-ia.webp)

**1. A streamlined hero section**
The hero was simplified significantly. The heavy illustration was replaced with a minimal one — present enough to add visual character, restrained enough not to compete with the CTA. With the visual noise removed, the primary action finally had room to breathe.

**2. A CTA that earns attention**
The CTA was elevated — placed in a highlighted container with soft contrasting colour to create immediate visual distinction. Not aggressive, but unmissable.

**3. A trust-building header**
Directly below the hero, a header section was added to surface Sirelo's key features and services. This section does two jobs: it builds user confidence quickly, and it communicates the platform's value before users scroll. It's also designed as a dynamic element — adaptable to different content depending on the audience or campaign.

![New homepage — improved hero section](/media/projects/sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey/sirelo-new-homepage.webp)

**4. A user-first content flow**
The page structure was re-ordered around what users actually want to know: what can Sirelo do for me, and how do I get started? Sections flow logically — starting with top movers in the user's area, then building outward to show the full range of what the platform offers.

**5. Secondary navigation moved to the footer**
The navigation banks weren't removed — they're still useful for users who want to explore — but they were relocated to the bottom of the page. Users who've already engaged with the hero and understood the value proposition are the right audience for that content. Moving it down keeps the top of the page focused.

![New navigation bank — repositioned to bottom of page](/media/projects/sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey/sirelo-nav-repositioned.gif)

![New navigation bank — repositioned to bottom of page](/media/projects/sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey/sirelo-nav-repositioned.webp)
**6. A softer colour palette**
The previous design used high-contrast colours throughout, which created visual fatigue and made it hard to establish hierarchy. The new palette uses soft, restrained background tones with two purposeful accent colours: blue for primary actions and user-facing CTAs, light cream for secondary sections. The contrast now guides attention rather than competing for it.

![New colour palette — soft accents, intentional hierarchy](/media/projects/sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey/sirelo-colour-palette.webp)

## Outcome

The redesigned homepage increased monthly page visits from 10,000 to 15,000 — a 50% uplift. Bounce rate dropped from 70% to 54%.

Both numbers moved because the design solved a real problem: users couldn't find the path to the action Sirelo needed them to take. Clearing that path — through a cleaner hero, a more prominent CTA, a better content flow, and a palette that establishes hierarchy — is what drove the results.
![New colour palette — soft accents, intentional hierarchy](/media/projects/sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey/sirelo-overview.gif)

## Key takeaways

**Information architecture does the heavy lifting.** Most of the meaningful design work here happened before a single visual was created. Getting the structure right made everything else easier.

**User-first sequencing.** Redesigning the content flow around the user's questions — not the platform's — is what made the page feel clearer. The information didn't change; the order did.

**Cross-disciplinary collaboration matters.** Having a product manager's strategic perspective and an SEO specialist's understanding of user intent alongside the design work meant every decision had multiple lenses on it. That combination produced a better result than any single discipline would have alone.

**Visual balance is a tool, not a preference.** The colour palette changes weren't aesthetic. They were functional — using contrast and tone to guide attention in a specific direction. When visual hierarchy is intentional, users don't have to think about where to look.
