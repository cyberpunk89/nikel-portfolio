## Overview

A year and a half working with Sagefund — a German fintech startup on a mission to make sustainable investing accessible to everyone. Starting from zero, I helped craft their brand identity, design system, and website. This is an account of how we approached it and what we learned along the way.

![Sagefund logo and title](/media/projects/sagefund-design-challenge/sagefund-title.webp)

---

## The challenge

Making investing feel approachable is harder than it sounds. Sagefund needed to occupy a genuinely difficult position in the market: serious enough to be trusted with people's money, warm enough to attract users who've never invested before, and clear enough to actually educate them along the way.

Three things had to coexist in the brand: *financial credibility*, *commitment to sustainability*, and *friendliness*. Most fintech brands pick one or two. Getting all three to feel coherent — without any of them undermining the others — was the central design challenge.

---

## Building the brand personality

Before any visual work, we needed to know what Sagefund actually stood for. I ran a series of sessions with the Sagefund team to surface their instincts about the brand from different angles — what it should feel like, who it was for, what it wasn't.

One of the most useful exercises was a word elimination process: we listed every word we could associate with the brand, then gradually cut them down until only the most essential ones remained. The pressure to eliminate forces clarity. You can't hide behind a long list of vague adjectives — you have to commit.

From those sessions, a brand persona emerged.

![Sagefund brand persona — traits and goals](/media/projects/sagefund-design-challenge/sagefund-brand-persona.webp)

The persona we landed on was a **teacher** — someone whose primary motivation is helping others understand and make good decisions, not projecting authority or expertise. This was a deliberate contrast to most financial products on the market, which position themselves as experts with little interest in education. Sagefund wanted to be the opposite of that.

Having the persona in place proved invaluable throughout the project. Whenever a design decision felt uncertain, we could ask: does this feel like something our persona would do? It gave us a consistent reference point across months of work.

---

## Moodboard and identity direction

With the brand personality defined, we moved into visual exploration — collecting references, building a moodboard, and starting to shape what the brand's visual identity should feel like.

![Moodboard design session](/media/projects/sagefund-design-challenge/sagefund-moodboard.webp)

The moodboard informed everything from typography to colour direction to the overall visual tone. From there, the full brand identity — logo, colour system, type choices, illustration style — was developed. The full identity process is documented on the Behance page.

## Design challenges

With brand identity in place, we moved to the website. Rather than walking through it chronologically, here are the five real design challenges we faced and how we solved them.

---

### 1. How bold should the design be?

Finance brands have a tendency to play it safe — neutral colours, conservative layouts, nothing that might feel out of place in a boardroom. The problem is that playing it safe means blending in. A forgettable brand is a liability when you're trying to build trust with first-time investors who are already cautious.

We took a considered risk. Alongside conventional corporate colours that signalled credibility, we introduced brighter, more energetic accent colours to create moments of contrast and grab attention where it mattered. The underlying principle: if you want to direct a user's attention, you have to create some friction — breaking expected patterns to highlight what's most important.

The same logic applied to page layout. Variation in layout structure keeps the user engaged; monotony loses them. We built variety into the page intentionally.

---

### 2. How do you communicate a complex product quickly?

The target audience — people new to investing — needed to understand what Sagefund was and why it was worth their time, fast. They might only read the first few sections. We had to make those count.

The first three sections (below the header) were designed as a self-contained narrative:

1. **Introduction** — What is Sagefund, and what makes it different?
2. **FAQ-style section** — Anticipating and answering the natural follow-up questions a new user would have after reading the intro
3. **Earnings calculator** — A tangible, interactive demonstration of the product's core benefit

After three sections, a user who hadn't scrolled further would still leave with a clear understanding of the platform. Everything below that was additional depth for the curious.

![First three sections of the Sagefund website](/media/projects/sagefund-design-challenge/sagefund-website-top-sections.webp)

---

### 3. What role did user feedback play?

Throughout the project, Sagefund maintained a beta-tester Facebook group of around 100 potential users. Every significant design decision — new layouts, logo iterations, colour schemes — was shared with the group for a vote.

We took the votes seriously, but not literally. Beta-testers consistently favoured safer, more familiar designs — which is a well-documented pattern. But their responses were still enormously useful as signal: what was confusing, what landed, where we'd lost them. The goal wasn't to give users exactly what they asked for; it was to understand what was and wasn't working in real time.

For Sagefund, being genuinely user-centric wasn't a nice-to-have — it was a core value. Testing iteratively with real users meant we weren't just making decisions we liked; we were making decisions that actually worked for the audience.

---

### 4. How far should the design go in educating the user?

Education was central to the brand persona. But education in UI is a balance — too little and users are confused, too much and they're overwhelmed or bored.

We started lean, with minimal copy, and tested it with the beta group. The feedback was clear: it wasn't enough. Users didn't feel they understood the product. So we expanded — adding sections, combining others, and creating external blog content to explain more complex concepts in depth.

The follow-up feedback showed users were satisfied. But an interesting insight emerged: not all users read every section, and many didn't need to. What mattered was that the information was *there if they wanted it*. Its existence made users feel safer, even when they didn't engage with it directly.

![Sustainability-focused sections](/media/projects/sagefund-design-challenge/sagefund-sustainability-sections.webp)

---

### 5. How do you explain numbers without overwhelming people?

The most technically challenging parts of the website were the sections dealing with potential earnings and fees. Numbers are where first-time investors tend to lose confidence. We needed them to feel simple and empowering, not intimidating.

Two interactive tools solved this.

**The earnings calculator** — Users input how much they're interested in investing and receive a real-time breakdown of their potential earnings and fees. We used dynamic text to surface the key numbers clearly, and avoided graphs unless they were genuinely necessary. Graphs can feel authoritative; they can also feel opaque.

![Earnings calculator](/media/projects/sagefund-design-challenge/sagefund-earnings-calculator.gif)

**The cost calculator** — An input slider that updates fee estimates in real time as users adjust their investment amount. Three pricing tiers each have a distinct colour, and that colour carries through to the fee display box — a visual connection that makes the tier system immediately legible. Most of Sagefund's competitors present this information as a table of numbers, asking users to do the calculation themselves. We did the opposite.

![Cost calculator](/media/projects/sagefund-design-challenge/sagefund-cost-calculator.gif)

## Outcome

The brand identity and website gave Sagefund a visual and strategic foundation that held up as the company grew. The design work — alongside the research and stakeholder presentations — contributed to the team securing **$400K in early-stage investment funding**.

Walking the line between serious, trustworthy, and friendly was the challenge from day one. A systematic approach to brand definition, continuous user testing, and a clear persona to test decisions against is what made it possible.
