## Background

Doula is a pregnancy app that provides audio guidance during the final phase of pregnancy — specifically to help users manage contraction pain. Alongside the audio experience, users can view statistics of their contractions. The app supports multiple languages to make it accessible to users across different countries.

---

## The challenge

The app was missing a symptom tracker — a tool expectant mothers could use to log their symptoms and monitor changes throughout their pregnancy.

On top of that, the visual style had grown dated and wasn't holding users' attention. People were reporting feeling disconnected from the app; they wanted something that felt more personal and supportive. Not just functional, but like something that was actually on their side.

Three things needed to happen:
- Introduce a symptom logging feature
- Give the app a modern visual overhaul
- Redesign the home screen to address the pain points users were hitting

## Research and understanding

Before touching any screens, I needed to understand what expectant mothers actually experience day-to-day — not just the clinical list of pregnancy symptoms, but how those symptoms feel to live with, how they vary, and what someone going through them would actually need from an app.

Morning sickness that makes food impossible. Fatigue that hits without warning. Cramping that's hard to contextualise without data. Each pregnancy is different, and that variability was the central design challenge: how do you build a feature that's useful across such a wide range of experiences?

![reserch pages](/media/projects/doula/reserch.png)

The research phase wasn't just background reading — it became the foundation for every design decision that followed. Understanding what users were going through made it possible to design something that felt genuinely useful rather than just feature-complete.

This research directly shaped the symptom tracker: not as a clinical logging form, but as a calm, accessible companion. Something that works at 3am when you're trying to make sense of how you're feeling and decide whether it's worth calling your doctor.

---

## Information architecture

The research fed into a thorough Information Architecture (IA) phase, which became the backbone of the entire design approach.

The IA gave me a bird's-eye view of the project scope — how the new features would integrate with what already existed in the app, where things needed to connect, and where the complexity was going to live.

The symptom logging feature turned out to be more layered than it first appeared. It wasn't just about capturing symptoms — it was about:

- Supporting the different ways users measure and experience their symptoms
- Making data entry fast, intuitive, and adaptable to individual preferences
- Giving users a clear way to view and interpret what they'd logged over time
- Enabling seamless sharing of that data with healthcare providers

![IA](/media/projects/doula/IA.png)

Each of those was its own design problem. The IA was where I mapped all of them before any screens were drawn.

**Summary of IA outcomes:**
- Research directly informed the structure of the new IA
- The IA served as a blueprint for integrating new features into the existing app
- Primary focus: a symptom logging feature that centred the user, not the data
- Key challenge: presenting diverse symptoms clearly and making input genuinely adaptable
- Design empowered users to view, interpret, and share their logged data
- Overarching goal: a supportive, reliable interface for expectant mothers

## Symptom tracker

### Home screen

![Home](/media/projects/doula/home.png)

The redesigned home screen is built around one idea: clarity without compromise.

The previous version had friction around the audio player — it wasn't obvious how to interact with it, which created confusion at a moment where users just wanted to relax. The new design makes every action self-evident, with brief in-context explanations that guide without overwhelming.

The audio player itself was rebuilt as a modular component — adaptable across all screen sizes and freeing up space for other features to coexist without crowding. The screen isn't fighting itself anymore.

A new **symptoms category** sits on the home screen, giving users immediate visibility into recently logged symptoms and any new reports generated from their data. Real-time, at a glance, without having to dig.

---

### Logging a symptom

![Logging a symptom](/media/projects/doula/symptoms.png)

The symptom logging flow was designed around one constraint: speed.

When someone needs to log a symptom, they're often mid-experience — uncomfortable, distracted, or just trying to quickly record something before it passes. The overlay-style interaction we designed keeps the logging action focused and contained. No navigating away from the home screen, no unnecessary steps.

The homepage only shows logged symptoms — a deliberate choice to keep the interface uncluttered. The homepage isn't an analytics dashboard; it's a quick-reference view. The deeper data lives in reports, where users can actually sit with it.

---

### Reports


![Logging a symptom](/media/projects/doula/reports.png)

The reports section is where logged symptoms become useful information.

Each symptom entry includes trend analysis — changes since the last report, displayed in a way that lets users quickly assess whether something is improving, worsening, or holding steady. The goal was to give users real-time data insights without requiring them to do the analysis themselves.

From the reports section, users can also connect directly with their healthcare providers through the integrated chat feature — a lower-friction alternative to trying to describe symptoms verbally over the phone.

---

### Sharing data with doctors

One of the most important features to get right was the ability to send symptom data directly to medical professionals.

Research pointed to a real problem on both sides: users were sending doctors more data than they could meaningfully process, and doctors were spending time filtering through it. The design addresses this by putting control firmly in the user's hands.

Users select which symptoms and reports they want to share — one by one, deliberately. They can flag reports before sending, review their selection, and send in a single action. The result is a more considered handoff: doctors receive what's actually relevant, and users feel in control of what they're sharing.

The chat feature gives users an additional channel for quick questions, without replacing the deeper async report-sharing flow.

**Summary of design decisions:**
- New design prioritises clarity and seamless guidance throughout
- Modular audio player adapts across all devices and makes room for other features
- Symptoms category on the home screen keeps users informed in real time
- Symptom logging uses an overlay flow — fast, focused, low-friction
- Home screen stays uncluttered by showing only logged symptoms
- Reports section provides in-depth trend analysis per symptom
- Users can share selected data with doctors directly from the app
- Deliberate symptom selection puts users in control of what they share
- Chat feature enables direct communication with healthcare professionals
- Every design decision serves both users and the doctors supporting them
