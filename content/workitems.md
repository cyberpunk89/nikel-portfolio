# 1. Doula — UX case study to upgrade a product

**Slug:** `doula`
**Year:** 2023
**Order:** 1

**Description:**
A full UX upgrade of a pregnancy companion app — from symptom tracking to screen-by-screen interaction redesign.

**My contribution:** Strategy · User research · Product design

**The team:** 1 × design director

**CTA:** [Read case study on Behance →](https://www.behance.net/gallery/182889757/Doula-UX-case-study-to-upgrade-a-product)

---

### Overview

DTT gave us a clear brief and we ran with it: take an existing pregnancy app and make it genuinely better — not just prettier, but more useful at the moments that actually matter to expectant mothers.

The goals were well-defined from the start. We needed to introduce a symptom-logging feature, give the app a modern visual overhaul, and fix a home screen that users kept getting stuck on. The harder goal — the one underneath all of those — was making the app feel personal and supportive, not clinical and generic.

We started where you have to start: with the people using it. Deep research into the day-to-day experience of expectant mothers — managing physical symptoms, finding moments of calm, navigating a lot of information that can feel overwhelming. That research became the foundation for a completely rethought information architecture.

The redesigned experience introduced a modular audio player, a dedicated symptoms category, and a user-friendly report system that lets users select and share symptom data directly with their healthcare providers. Less friction for users; less load on doctors. We also built in real-time data insights and an easy pathway to connect with healthcare professionals when something felt off.

---

### Process

**Starting with research, not assumptions.**
We spent the first phase in discovery — mapping the experience of pregnancy through user interviews and behavioural analysis. What are the high-anxiety moments? Where does the app fall short when it matters most? The answers shaped everything that followed.

**Rethinking the information architecture.**
The original home screen tried to do too much at once. We stripped it back and rebuilt it around a clearer hierarchy: what does a user need right now, today, at this stage of their pregnancy? That question drove every structural decision.

**Designing the symptom log.**
This was the most technically and emotionally nuanced part of the project. A symptom logger for pregnant women isn't a form — it's a conversation. We designed it to feel low-effort, reassuring, and genuinely useful rather than another thing to fill in. The report-sharing feature meant that data didn't just disappear into the app — it could become part of a real medical conversation.

**Visual redesign.**
The app needed a visual refresh that felt warm and modern without being infantilising. We developed a design language that balanced calm with energy — something that could hold space for the full emotional range of pregnancy.

---

### Outcome

A redesigned app experience that addressed the core pain points identified in research, introduced meaningful new features, and delivered a visual identity that felt cohesive and considered. The symptom log and report-sharing feature in particular were direct responses to real user needs — and the kind of thing that makes a product genuinely useful rather than just well-designed.

# 2. Navigating Clutter to Clarity — Sirelo Homepage Redesign

**Slug:** `sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey`
**Year:** 2023
**Order:** 2

**Description:**
A full homepage redesign for Sirelo — Europe's leading moving company comparison platform — that turned a cluttered, low-converting page into something that actually worked.

**My contribution:** UX/UI design · UX research

**The team:** 1 × product manager · 1 × SEO specialist · 1 × UX designer

**CTA:** [Read full case study on Medium →](https://medium.com/muzli-design-inspiration/navigating-clutter-to-clarity-sirelo-homepage-redesign-journey-294aaa15ce02)

---

### Overview

Sirelo is a platform that helps people find and compare moving companies. The product itself is solid — but the homepage wasn't doing it justice. A cluttered layout, vague calls-to-action, and a value proposition that wasn't landing were all getting in the way of users doing what they came to do: find a mover and request a quote.

Working with a product manager and an SEO specialist, I led the UX and design work on a full homepage redesign. The goal wasn't just to make it look better — it was to make the page meaningfully more useful. That meant rethinking the information architecture from scratch, simplifying the user flow, and writing the visual hierarchy so that every element was pulling in the same direction.

---

### Process

**Understanding what was broken.**
The starting point was an audit — what was the page actually communicating, and was it working? Cluttered design, unclear CTAs, and a value proposition that required too much reading were the headline issues. We also looked at where users were dropping off and what the SEO data was telling us about intent.

**Rebuilding the information architecture.**
Once we had a clear picture of the problems, we restructured the homepage around user intent. What does someone arriving on this page actually need to know, in what order, to take the next step? That became the skeleton of the new design.

**Design and iteration.**
Wireframes, prototypes, feedback loops with the team. The visual work focused on streamlining the hero, sharpening the CTA, and creating a cleaner path to quote requests. We also worked closely with the SEO specialist to make sure the content changes didn't undermine search performance — design and discoverability had to coexist.

---

### Outcome

The redesigned homepage increased monthly page visits from 10,000 to 15,000 — a 50% uplift. Bounce rate dropped from 70% to 54%. Both numbers moved in the right direction because the design was grounded in a real understanding of the user, not just an aesthetic refresh.

Published as a full case study in Muzli Design Inspiration on Medium.

# 3. Sagefund — Design Challenge

**Slug:** `sagefund-design-challenge`
**Year:** 2019
**Order:** 2

**Description:**
A behind-the-scenes look at the design challenges behind building a sustainable fintech brand from scratch.

**My contribution:** Brand design · User research · UI/UX design

**The team:** 2 × product owners · 3 × engineers

**CTA:** [Read more on Behance →](https://www.behance.net/gallery/125865595/Sagefund-Design-challenge)

---

### Overview

Sagefund is a German fintech startup with a clear mission: make sustainable investing simple and accessible for everyone. For a year and a half, I worked embedded in their team — shaping the brand identity, solving design challenges as they surfaced, and helping the product grow from early concept to something real.

This case study is a honest account of how I approached the design work and what I learned along the way. Not just the polished output — but the thinking, the pivots, and the problems that didn't have obvious solutions.

---

### Process

**Finding the brand's voice.**
Sustainable investing sits in an awkward space: serious enough to require trust, approachable enough to attract first-time investors who've been put off by traditional finance. The challenge was designing a brand that felt both credible and human — rigorous without being cold.

**Working through the design challenges.**
Over the course of the engagement, I tackled a range of problems: how to communicate complex ESG information without overwhelming users, how to design onboarding flows that build confidence rather than anxiety, and how to maintain visual consistency as the product evolved.

**Learning by doing.**
This project taught me a lot about working in an early-stage environment — where the brief changes, resources are tight, and you're often the only designer in the room. You learn to make decisions faster, communicate your reasoning more clearly, and hold the design vision even when everything else is shifting.

---

### Outcome

A design system and brand language that held up as Sagefund grew. The project also contributed to the team securing $400K in investment funding — design as a signal of credibility and intentionality, not just aesthetics.

# 4. CoorpID — Design Challenge

**Slug:** `coorpid-design-challenge`
**Year:** 2021
**Order:** 3

**Description:**
A service design challenge to rethink the onboarding experience for CoorpID — an ING Bank identity verification platform for businesses.

**My contribution:** UX design · Service design · Conducting design sprints

**The team:** 2 × communication designers · 1 × UI/UX designer · 2 × business and service designers

**CTA:** [Read case study on Medium →](https://medium.com/@nikel_design/case-study-designing-a-onboading-system-fc273d6a916c)

---

### Overview

During my master's program at the University of Twente, I took part in a design challenge hosted by CoorpID — a platform that handles identity verification and onboarding for business clients, operating under ING Bank.

The brief was focused but demanding: redesign the onboarding process for their platform in a way that could be implemented into their strategy immediately. Not a blue-sky concept — something real and actionable.

I was part of an interdisciplinary team of five: alongside four classmates from communication design and business and service design. Two months. Real stakeholders. Real constraints.

---

### Process

**Cross-disciplinary collaboration.**
Working with people from different backgrounds is genuinely different from working in a design team. Communication designers think about message and medium. Business designers think about value and feasibility. Finding the common language — and the shared definition of success — was itself a design challenge.

**Running design sprints.**
We used design sprints to move quickly and test ideas before investing in them. Sprint structure helped keep five opinionated people aligned and gave us a clear framework for making decisions under time pressure.

**Mapping the onboarding experience.**
CoorpID's onboarding process was complex — involving multiple stakeholders, document verification, and compliance requirements. We mapped the full service journey to identify where friction was highest and where small changes could have the biggest impact.

**Prototyping and presenting.**
We built prototypes, tested them, and refined our approach. The final proposal was presented directly to the CoorpID team — a solution designed to slot into their existing strategy without requiring a full platform rebuild.

---

### Outcome

A service design proposal that addressed the core friction points in CoorpID's onboarding process, grounded in user research and structured for immediate implementation. A strong lesson in what happens when design, business, and communication work from the same table.

# 5. Efficiency and Fun — Designing a Shipping Calculator

**Slug:** `sirelo-interaction-design`
**Year:** 2023
**Order:** 3

**Description:**
Redesigning a shipping quote tool into something users actually wanted to engage with — without sacrificing clarity or conversion.

**My contribution:** UI/UX design · Interaction design · Prototyping

**The team:** 1 × product manager

**CTA:** [See full case study on Behance →](https://www.behance.net/gallery/176492715/Efficiency-and-Fun-Designing-a-shipping-calculator)

---

### Overview

Tools don't have to be boring. That was the premise here.

The shipping calculator had a core problem — users had to select a starting location before they could even see destination options, and most of them didn't realise that's what they needed to do. The interface was hiding a required decision, and the drop-off showed it.

The challenge: fix the underlying UX problem, streamline the path to a quote request, and do it in a way that felt engaging rather than transactional. Working with one product manager, I led the design and interaction work from problem framing through to a tested prototype.

---

### Process

**Naming the problem.**
We called the core issue the "Invisible Hand" — users were being guided by a constraint they couldn't see. Surfacing that constraint clearly, without making the interface feel heavier, was the first design problem to solve.

**Introducing the shipping toggle.**
The solution was a shipping-from toggle with distinct states — prompting users to make an active, visible choice before moving forward. A small change with a meaningful impact on how users understood the flow.

**Replacing the table with an interactive calculator.**
Static tables let users scan data quickly — but they weren't giving users what they actually needed: a clear estimate for their specific route, with a natural next step. The interactive calculator traded quick scanning for real-time, personalised estimates. Designed carefully so the shift felt like a gain, not a loss.

**Adding personality without distraction.**
Small boat animations gave the calculator a bit of character. The kind of thing you notice and smile at, then move past — it doesn't interfere with the task, but it makes the experience feel designed rather than assembled.

---

### Outcome

A redesigned calculator that addressed the core UX friction, shortened the path to quote requests, and demonstrated that a functional tool and an enjoyable experience aren't mutually exclusive. Anticipated to drive significant improvements in user engagement and conversion.

# 6. BBC News App — UX Case Study

**Slug:** `bbc`
**Year:** 2018
**Order:** 4

**Description:**
One of my earliest case studies — a UX redesign of the BBC News Android app, published on Medium and read by 12,000+ people.

**My contribution:** UX research · Prototype

**CTA:** [Read case study on Medium →](https://medium.muz.li/ux-case-study-bbc-news-app-android-7de05781413b)

---

### Overview

This was one of my first projects where I documented the full design process end-to-end. The BBC News Android app was the subject: a product with enormous reach and high daily usage, but with some clear usability friction that I wanted to explore.

I conducted UX research, mapped out the problems, proposed a redesigned experience, and built a prototype of the proposed application. The work was published in the Muzli Design Inspiration publication on Medium and went on to receive over 12,000 views.

Looking back, this project taught me that thorough documentation and honest process writing makes design work accessible to other designers — and that sharing your thinking publicly is one of the best ways to sharpen it.

---

### Process

**Research and audit.**
I started with a hands-on audit of the existing app — mapping navigation patterns, identifying usability friction, and noting where the experience felt inconsistent. I supplemented this with secondary research into how news app users consume content and what they prioritise.

**Defining the problems.**
The key friction points centred on navigation clarity, content hierarchy, and the discoverability of features. A news app lives or dies by how quickly and confidently users can get to the content they want.

**Designing the solution.**
With clear problem statements in place, I moved into ideation and design. The proposed redesign focused on a cleaner navigation structure, a stronger content hierarchy, and interaction patterns that felt familiar enough to be comfortable but improved enough to be meaningfully better.

**Prototyping.**
A prototype of the redesigned experience was built to demonstrate the proposed flows. Putting the design into a clickable prototype forces you to confront decisions you'd otherwise gloss over — which made the final proposal stronger.

---

### Outcome

A published UX case study that reached 12,000+ readers through Muzli Design Inspiration. One of the pieces that shaped how I think about documenting and communicating design work — and the project that made me realise how much I enjoy writing about process, not just doing it.

# 7. Beautiful Homes — UI/UX Design

**Slug:** `beautiful-homes`
**Year:** 2019
**Order:** 4

**Description:**
Designing a dynamic desktop experience for Asian Paints' Beautiful Homes — a platform for home décor inspiration and education.

**My contribution:** Layout design · User research · Prototype

**The team:** 1 × product owner · 1 × UX designer

**CTA:** [Read more on Behance →](https://www.behance.net/gallery/84990457/UX-design-for-Beautiful-homes)

---

### Overview

Beautiful Homes is a website from Asian Paints for people who want to explore home décor ideas — a mix of inspiration, education, and practical guidance for making a home feel like a home.

I completed this project during my internship at Redd Experience Design in Bangalore. I was involved in the full design process, working closely with a senior colleague who supervised the project. My primary focus was the desktop version, with some involvement in the mobile version during its final stages.

The brief was specific: build a desktop experience that genuinely complemented the mobile version — not a scaled-up copy, but something that took advantage of the desktop canvas without being constrained by the mobile layout. The desktop version had freedom in structure and layout that the mobile version didn't.

---

### Process

**Understanding the users.**
People coming to Beautiful Homes are in a particular mindset — exploratory, visually driven, often gathering ideas rather than making immediate decisions. The design needed to support browsing and discovery rather than task completion. That shapes everything from layout to navigation to how imagery is handled.

**Layout and visual hierarchy.**
The desktop canvas gave us room to work with. We used it to create a richer, more editorial layout — prioritising imagery and letting content breathe in ways that mobile couldn't. The challenge was keeping that openness coherent, making sure the layout didn't feel empty or arbitrary.

**Prototyping and iteration.**
We built and tested prototypes to validate the layout decisions, checking that the experience held up across different content types — inspiration galleries, how-to articles, product showcases. Different content needs different treatment; the design system had to be flexible enough to handle all of it.

---

### Outcome

A desktop experience that expanded on the mobile version rather than just mirroring it — giving users a richer canvas for exploration without losing the coherence of the overall platform. A strong early lesson in designing for different screen contexts with the same underlying intent.

# 8. Sagefund — Brand Identity

**Slug:** `sagefund-branding`
**Year:** 2019
**Order:** 5

**Description:**
Building the brand identity for Sagefund from scratch — a sustainable investment platform on a mission to make investing accessible.

**My contribution:** Brand design · User research · Product design

**The team:** 2 × product owners · 3 × engineers

**CTA:** [Read more on Behance →](https://www.behance.net/gallery/97492349/Sagefund-Brand-Identity)

---

### Overview

Sagefund is a digital wealth management platform built around sustainable investing. The goal is simple to state and hard to execute: make investing safe, easy, and accessible to people who've never done it before — while staying compliant with ESG and UN Global Compact goals.

I came into the project at the start, which meant working on the brand identity before there was a product to design for. That's a different kind of challenge — you're not solving a UX problem yet, you're defining what the brand stands for and what that should look, feel, and sound like. Everything that comes after has to be consistent with those early decisions.

---

### Process

**Defining the brand position.**
Sagefund needed to sit in a specific space: credible enough to be trusted with someone's money, approachable enough not to intimidate first-time investors. Traditional finance brands lean hard into authority and formality. We wanted something different — warmer, more human, but still rigorous.

**Research into the audience.**
We looked at how first-time retail investors think about money and investing. The anxieties, the misconceptions, the moments of confusion that make people put it off. The brand needed to speak directly to that — to be the thing that makes it feel manageable.

**Building the identity.**
Logo, colour palette, typography, visual language. Each decision was made with the dual audience in mind: the user who needs to trust the brand, and the investor or partner who needs to take it seriously. Getting both in the same design system without compromising either was the central tension of the work.

---

### Outcome

A brand identity that held up across contexts — product UI, marketing materials, investor presentations. The design work was part of a broader effort that contributed to Sagefund securing $400K in early-stage investment funding.

# 10. Crossbeats — UI/UX Design

**Slug:** `cross-beats`
**Year:** 2019

**Description:**
Designing a high-energy web experience for Crossbeats — an audio brand targeting active, youth-focused lifestyles.

**My contribution:** UI design · User research · Prototype

**The team:** 1 × product manager · 2 × product designers

**CTA:** [Read more on Behance →](https://www.behance.net/gallery/84990729/UX-design-for-Crossbeats)

---

### Overview

Crossbeats makes audio equipment for people who don't sit still — earbuds, headphones, and gear designed for an active lifestyle. The brand's focus demographic is young, movement-oriented, and used to high-quality digital experiences.

The brief was to create a web design that embodied that energy — bold, clean, and youth-facing — while developing new engagement features that would keep users connected to the brand ecosystem. I worked on this during my internship as part of a three-person design team.

One constraint shaped everything: all proposed designs had to be implementable on the platform Crossbeats was already using for development. Creative freedom with a firm technical ceiling — which, it turns out, is a good design condition to work in.

---

### Process

**Defining the visual direction.**
High-energy and clean don't always coexist naturally in web design. We spent time working out what "bold simplicity" looked like for Crossbeats specifically — not generic sports-brand aesthetics, but something with its own point of view.

**User research.**
We looked at the target demographic: what they respond to, how they navigate brand sites, what builds affinity versus what feels like noise. The research fed into both the visual choices and the interaction patterns.

**Developing new engagement features.**
Beyond the core design, we explored new ways for users to interact with the brand — experiences that would keep them in the ecosystem rather than bouncing after a single visit. I was excited to experiment here; it was the part of the brief with the most creative latitude.

**Staying within platform constraints.**
Every design decision went through a feasibility check against the development platform. This was a useful discipline: it forced us to be specific about how things would actually work, rather than designing in the abstract and leaving implementation to engineering.

---

### Outcome

A web design concept for Crossbeats that balanced brand energy with usability — clean aesthetics, high-impact visuals, and engagement features designed for the platform's actual users. A strong early lesson in designing within real constraints without letting them flatten the creative work.
